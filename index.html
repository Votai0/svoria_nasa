Beginners Guide

What is an exoplanet?

An exoplanet (a.k.a. extrasolar planet) is a planet that orbits a star other than our Sun.

We've now confirmed thousands of them. They come in many types - hot Jupiters (giant, very close-in), super-Earths (rocky worlds bigger than Earth), and mini-Neptunes (puffy, sub-Neptune worlds) - showing that planetary systems are wildly diverse.

How are exoplanets found?

Transit method

  • Transit - the planet passes in front of its star and blocks a tiny bit of light; brightness dips in a repeating pattern.

Kepler and TESS lean heavily on transit detections because they can monitor thousands of stars at once and look for repeating dips.

Quick vocabulary

  • Flux: measured brightness of a star.
  • Light curve: flux vs. time plot.
  • Transit depth: how deep the dip is; roughly ≈ (planet radius / star radius)2.
  • Period: time from one transit to the next.
  • Duration: how long the transit lasts.
  • Ingress / egress: the start and end parts of the dip.

How to read a light curve fast

Planet-like signals

  • Evenly spaced, repeating dips (constant period).
  • Rounded "U" bottoms for small planets + quiet stars.
  • Depth consistent across transits (ignoring noise).

Common false positives

  • V-shaped dips (can be grazing eclipsing binaries).
  • Odd-even depth differences (binary stars).
  • Dips only in some sectors/cameras (systematics).
